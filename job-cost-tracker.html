Most contractors don't lose money in big dramatic ways.

They lose it quietly. A foreman forgets to log the verbal change order the owner asked for on Tuesday. A gas receipt gets stuffed in a truck console and nobody ever codes it to a job. An excavator sits idle for three days on a site that's already been cleared, and the rental meter keeps running.

Nobody sees any of it until the bookkeeper closes the month and asks "why did Lemon Avenue come in twelve grand under quoted margin?" By then it's too late to fix.

That's the problem JCT solves.

The Four Leaks

Where your margin actually goes.

Top-quartile contractors keep these to 6 to 8 points of margin. Bottom-quartile run 14 to 18. The gap is $8,000 of lost margin for every $100,000 of materials. On a $10M year, that's $200,000 to $300,000 hiding in plain sight.

Verbal change orders

The owner shows up at 2pm on Thursday and says "while you're here, can you regrade this corner?" The foreman says yes. The crew does it. Nobody writes it down. Three weeks later the job closes under quote and nobody remembers the extra work. Unbilled and verbal COs eat 2 to 3% of annual revenue on average.

Misallocated crew hours

Juan was at Lemon Avenue four hours and Oak Road three hours, but the foreman logs it all to one job. The other job silently eats the cost. A 10% labor overrun typically wipes out the entire net margin on a job.

Receipts in the wrong bucket

Fuel card receipts, material pickups, disposal tickets, rental invoices. These pile up for weeks before a bookkeeper codes them. When there's no job tag, they land in overhead and quietly eat portfolio margin instead of showing up on the job that caused them.

Equipment idle and rework

Heavy equipment idles 40 to 50% of its runtime industry-wide. An excavator sitting on a cleared site for three days costs the same as one doing real work. Same thing for rework. Inspectors flag an issue, the crew goes back to fix it, and nobody asks whether the cost is billable or absorbed.

How It Works

A phone number. That's the whole tool.

Five-minute setup. No app to install. No new process for the crew. It works because it replaces the paperwork they were already doing.

Setup in five minutes

Connect QuickBooks through Intuit. Add phone numbers for your foremen, ops manager, and yourself. They save the number in their contacts as "JCT." That's the whole setup.

Foremen text what happened

Snap a receipt photo and text it. Say "owner asked for extra regrade on east corner, maybe $500." Say "Kubota leaking oil, took it to the shop." Plain English. No forms, no dropdowns.

AI reads, tags, and files it

It figures out which job the hours, receipts, and materials belong to. Drafts the change orders the owner forgot to bill. Everything flows to QuickBooks automatically, tagged to the right job.

End-of-day pulse

At 5pm the AI texts each foreman: "How'd the day go? Crew hours on each job, anything unusual, anything the owner asked for." Foreman types the way they'd text their boss. Under a minute total.

One text Friday to approve the week

Every Friday at 4pm the AI sends a full week summary. Foreman replies OK and the whole week locks, pushes to QuickBooks as the right record types. Expenses, time activities, draft invoices, bills. All tagged.

Bookkeeper opens clean books

Every other week, the bookkeeper opens QuickBooks and finds everything already categorized. No chasing receipts. No guessing at job codes. No double entry.

Who Can Text It

Three roles. Every entry traces back to a name.

Only three phone numbers can submit. Crew members can't. If they try, they get an auto-reply telling them to hand the receipt to their foreman. Every entry in the system is tied to a whitelisted phone.

Foreman

Runs the crew on a job. Sends the daily pulse, crew hours, and any receipts they personally paid for that day.

Ops Manager

Runs multiple foremen. Handles cross-job receipts, rentals, subcontractor invoices, and trucking that isn't tied to a single crew.

Owner

Big purchases, approvals, reviews. Sees the whole picture in the dashboard. Gets flagged when something needs attention.

Why the crew actually uses it

Construction has a no-rat code. JCT doesn't ask "what went wrong today." It asks "what added time" or "what changed from plan." Answers are neutral. Rock, weather, equipment, spec issue, supplier. The owner sees "equipment incident, $420 repair, down 1 day" without a crew member's name attached. Foremen tell the AI the truth because the AI isn't framed as a surveillance tool. It replaces the paperwork they were already doing and keeps the crew out of the blame game.
